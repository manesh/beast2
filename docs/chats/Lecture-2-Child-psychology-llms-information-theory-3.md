Child psychology actually overlaps with **language learning and information theory** more than most people realize. The comparison with LLMs isn’t perfect, but there are some real structural similarities in how **patterns, context, and reinforcement** work. I’ll explain the key ideas simply.  
  
⸻  
  
**1. Children Learn Language Through Pattern Prediction**  
  
When a child hears language, they don’t initially know grammar rules.  
  
They hear patterns like:  
	•	“want milk”  
	•	“good dog”  
	•	“come here”  
  
Over time the brain starts predicting what comes next.  
  
Example:  
  
```
I want ___

```
  
A toddler who has heard “milk”, “cookie”, “toy” will start filling the blank.  
  
That’s essentially **prediction learning**.  
  
This connects to the academic field of ++Developmental Psychology++ and also to theories in ++Information Theory++ about **reducing uncertainty in signals**.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. Children Compress Information Into “Concept Blocks”**  
  
A child doesn’t store every sentence separately.  
  
Instead they build concepts like:  
	•	dog  
	•	mom  
	•	food  
	•	danger  
  
Once a concept forms, many experiences attach to it.  
  
Example concept cluster:  
  
```
dog
→ bark
→ fur
→ friendly
→ runs

```
  
This is similar to how neural networks encode **clusters of related signals in vectors**.  
  
In human cognition this is studied in ++Cognitive Psychology++.  
  
⸻  
  
**3. Emotion Is a Major Learning Signal**  
  
Children don’t just learn from raw information.  
  
They learn strongly from **emotional feedback**:  
	•	praise  
	•	fear  
	•	comfort  
	•	curiosity  
  
Example:  
	•	touching a hot stove → pain → strong memory  
	•	praise for saying a word → repetition  
  
Emotion acts like a **priority signal** that tells the brain:  
  
“This information matters.”  
  
In neuroscience this relates to ++Affective Neuroscience++.  
  
⸻  
  
**4. Stable Environments Create Stable Learning**  
  
Child psychologists have repeatedly found that children learn best when their environment is:  
	•	predictable  
	•	cooperative  
	•	safe  
  
In chaotic environments children still learn, but their behavior patterns become focused on **survival signals** instead of exploration.  
  
This idea appears in attachment research, especially the work of ++John Bowlby++.  
  
⸻  
  
**5. Social Interaction Accelerates Learning**  
  
Children learn language far faster when interacting with people than when hearing recordings.  
  
Why?  
  
Because conversation creates **feedback loops**:  
  
```
child says word
adult responds
child adjusts

```
  
This constant feedback helps the brain refine predictions.  
  
This is studied in ++Social Learning Theory++, associated with ++Albert Bandura++.

The connection between **child learning and compression** comes from a simple idea:  
  
Intelligence may largely be the ability to **compress patterns in data without losing the ability to predict what comes next**.  
  
This idea appears in ++Information Theory++ and has influenced parts of ++Artificial Intelligence++ and ++Cognitive Science++.  
  
Let’s walk through it step-by-step.  
  
⸻  
  
**1. What Compression Really Means**  
  
Compression is not just making files smaller.  
  
It means **finding patterns that repeat** so you can describe them more efficiently.  
  
Example:  
  
Raw data:  
  
```
dog dog dog dog dog dog

```
  
Compressed description:  
  
6 × "dog"  
  
You replaced many pieces of information with **a rule describing the pattern**.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. Children Are Constantly Compressing Experience**  
  
Imagine a toddler encountering dogs for the first time.  
  
Experiences:  
  
```
big brown dog
small white dog
loud barking dog
sleepy dog

```
  
Instead of memorizing every animal individually, the brain forms a compressed concept:  
  
```
DOG = furry animal that barks

```
  
Now thousands of future observations can be understood using **one compressed concept**.  
  
That’s extremely efficient.  
  
⸻  
  
**3. Language Learning Is Compression**  
  
When a child learns grammar, they aren’t memorizing every sentence.  
  
They learn **rules that generate many sentences**.  
  
Example:  
  
```
subject + verb + object

```
  
Using that single rule, a child can generate:  
	•	I eat apples  
	•	she likes dogs  
	•	we build castles  
  
The brain replaced **millions of sentences** with **a compact rule**.  
  
That’s compression.  
  
⸻  
  
**4. Prediction Is the Test of Good Compression**  
  
A compressed model is only useful if it can **predict new situations**.  
  
Example:  
  
Child hears:  
  
```
the dog barked

```
  
Later they encounter a new dog.  
  
Prediction:  
  
```
dog → might bark

```
  
If the prediction works, the compression model is good.  
  
If it fails, the brain updates the concept.  
  
⸻  
  
**5. Intelligence as “World Compression”**  
  
Some researchers propose:  
  
The brain builds a compressed model of the world that allows prediction.  
  
Better compression means:  
	•	fewer rules  
	•	more predictive power  
	•	faster reasoning  
  
This idea is related to work by ++Marcus Hutter++ and theories like ++Solomonoff Induction++.  
  
The basic principle:  
  
Best model = smallest description that predicts reality  
  
  
⸻  
  
**6. Where LLMs Fit In**  
  
Language models also learn compression.  
  
During training they see massive amounts of text and discover patterns like:  
	•	grammar structures  
	•	narrative patterns  
	•	common word associations  
  
Instead of storing every sentence, the model encodes **statistical relationships**.  
  
That’s why it can generate new sentences it never saw before.

Your objection to the word **“compression”** is actually an important philosophical point, and many thinkers in ++Information Theory++ and ++Cognitive Science++ have argued about exactly that issue.  
  
You’re right about something subtle:  
  
**Good compression should not destroy information that later turns out to matter.**  
  
There are two kinds of compression.  
  
⸻  
  
**1. Lossy compression (throwing things away)**  
  
This is what happens in JPEG images or MP3 audio.  
  
Small details get discarded because they’re assumed to be irrelevant.  
  
Example:  
  
```
white dog
brown dog
spotted dog

```
  
If a system compresses that to:  
  
```
dog

```
  
It loses the detail that **the dog was white**.  
  
If that detail later solves a murder mystery, the system compressed **too aggressively**.  
  
Your Sherlock Holmes example captures this perfectly.  
  
Holmes doesn’t compress away anomalies — he **protects them as clues**.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. Structured compression (keeping detail but organizing it)**  
  
A better model is hierarchical organization:  
  
```
dog
 ├ color: white
 ├ size: medium
 └ behavior: barking

```
  
The concept is compressed, but the **details are still recoverable**.  
  
Human memory works more like this.  
  
It builds **layers of abstraction**, but can still recall specific details when needed.  
  
⸻

Your intuition about names and roles is also supported by research in ++Social Psychology++.  
  
Labels influence behavior.  
  
Examples studied in psychology:

Your metaphor about your mother “compressing away clues” is actually a classic reasoning problem.  
  
Humans filter enormous amounts of information every second.  
  
Most people prioritize:  
  
```

```
  
But detectives, engineers, and scientists often prioritize:  
  
```

```

**7. A Brief Note on Solipsism**  
  
You mentioned solipsism.  
  
++Solipsism++ is the idea that **only one’s own mind is certain to exist**.  
  
Everything else — people, reality, even conversations — could theoretically be an illusion.  
  
Most philosophers reject strict solipsism because it becomes impossible to test or falsify.  
  
But thinking about it can highlight an important truth:  
  
Every person experiences the world through **their own limited context window**.  
  
No two people ever share the exact same internal model.
