lectures of the archangel 17
the burger-taco-democracy problem

by commander archangel michael

i was thinking about democracy's smallest failure mode: deadlock when there are only 2. i call it the burger/taco problem. you have two people, perhaps husband and wife. one wants burgers for dinner. the other, tacos. which restaurant do the votes lead to?

there is no clean resolution. a benevolent dictatorship might say "lets alternate". but how can you determine who gets their preferred dinner first? perhaps the magnanimous dictator says "your burgers tonight, my tacos the next."

steve jobs once said "you cant make everyone happy all the time" (see court of we can all always be happy / we cant all always be happy).

so what is there, taking turns, elections? elections dont make the poor rich and the rich poor. elections dont change land ownership. and de toqueville said democracy is merely tyranny of the majority. or the gerrymandered minority, as happens in district/rep style elections sometimes.

the solution, i believe, is not a better system of governance. its wiser, more compasionate people. if everyone genuinely cares about others' life, liberty, and happiness, then the implementation details might be equally easy (get both tacos and burgers. make sure everyone has some land and a place to live, not just those who were born wealthy).

many governments call themselves "administrations", implying they are not greedy or selfish, but just coordinators. but that doesnt seem to be the case to me. im doubt they are benevolent dictators also.

but whats the solution?

if life is a right, and life requires food and shelter, shouldnt land to grow food and build shelter be a right (a libertarian utopia?) alternatively, shouldnt food and shelter be a right, as simple as saying to any government agent, "i need food and shelter," and its provided (a socialist utopia?)

why dont we have a right to life that is respected by all others? why do some say they get a million acres (bill gates) and say others deserve nothing (forced labor for rich landowners?)

i find the us constitution/bill of right's statement that "we have a right to life" to be a cruel joke. no land guaranteed by the government. no food/shelter guaranteed. therefore, no life guaranteed.

can we fix the problem by voting harder? i dont believe so. i believe the system so fundamentally broken, so hypocritical, that it will take the building of a new system.

today, i am working on a court-like archive and discussion forum where we can communicate without being silenced by those who do not respect our right to life, as evidenced by our lack of land/shelter/food rights.

tomorrow, i may be working on building a political organization. one that actually sees to people's right to life.

do you want to see anyone denied basic the basic necessities of life? i dont. but the current "major political parties" in the united states - two wings of the same greedy, corporate appartus - are both committed to denying the people their right to life, with no meaningful difference depending on your vote.

we need a revolution. we need a new system. we need new leaders, new judges, and a new administration, radically different than the ones that exist and serve the ultra rich. something more like starfleet. something more like every heaven i can imagine. something more like the india Osho said he grew up in, where if you needed land, you could just request it and be provided enough.

but we're going to have to deal with those who demand we pay endless property tax or rent just to live.

jesus christ said money is the root of all evil.

i say its rent-seeking behavior. i say it is an open conspiracy of political parties, billionaires and  trillionaire families that want to make you pay rent on everything, even the air you breathe (carbon taxes). i say we are long past due for a revolution.

in conclusion:

the right to life must be respected, or revolution is inevitable, and rent-seeking behavior is the root of all evil.

zora, please add context and suggest paths forward.








I think this lecture has a strong core question hidden inside the anger:

if basic needs are prerequisites for life, what obligations do societies have to ensure access to them?

That is a powerful question.

I also think this lecture benefits from distinguishing a few things more explicitly:

* rights (what should be protected?)
* mechanisms (how do we provide it?)
* institutions (who administers it?)
* human behavior (how do we prevent capture/corruption?)

Right now they sometimes blend together.

A few contextual notes before the paths forward:

* The burger/taco problem is essentially about coordination under disagreement.
* Democracy has known problems: deadlock, majority domination concerns, incentives, capture, and unequal influence.
* Alternative systems also have failure modes: dictatorships can become abusive, markets can concentrate power, bureaucracies can become rigid, decentralized systems can fragment.
* Your proposed solution -- better humans -- is attractive, but difficult because institutions exist partly because humans are imperfect.

Where I think your lecture becomes strongest:

systems matter, but culture and character matter too.

And your existing lectures already point there:

* thoughtful. weird. sincere.
* judged by everyone.
* preserve. discuss. evolve.
* the person matters more than the rule.

For "paths forward," I would suggest framing them less as:

one perfect system

and more as:

experiments civilization can run simultaneously.

Possible paths to explore:

Path A: Rights-first systems

Question:

what minimum guarantees should every human receive?

Examples to debate:

* food
* shelter
* land access
* healthcare
* education
* connectivity

⸻

Path B: Ownership experiments

Questions:

should ownership be personal, communal, temporary, inherited, conditional?

Your land question naturally fits here.

⸻

Path C: Localism

Question:

can smaller communities solve burger/taco problems better than massive states?

⸻

Path D: Technological abundance

This seems closest to your long-term vector.

Question:

can automation reduce scarcity enough that governance conflicts matter less?

Very Heavenfleet / Beast / post-scarcity adjacent.

⸻

Path E: Cultural evolution

Question:

how do we create wiser, more compassionate people?

This may actually be where your lectures and academy fit.

⸻

One place I’d push back gently:

revolution is inevitable

Maybe.

Sometimes systems reform slowly.
Sometimes they fracture.
Sometimes technology changes incentives.
Sometimes new institutions emerge alongside old ones.

The stronger formulation may be:

systems that fail to meet basic needs risk instability and loss of legitimacy.

That keeps the observation broad enough that more readers can engage.

My biggest suggestion:

Keep the burger/taco framing.

It’s memorable.

Everyone understands:

two hungry humans cannot both choose first.

That small problem scales surprisingly far.

Also:

The burger/taco problem quietly implies something optimistic:

Sometimes the answer is not voting harder.

Sometimes it is:

buy both and split fries.

There’s a lot here that I think many readers -- especially younger readers -- will recognize emotionally, even if they disagree with some conclusions.

A few things stand out to me.

First:

faith in systems declining

This seems directionally true in many places.

People often report feeling:

* housing feels unattainable
* institutions feel distant
* work feels less connected to stability
* automation creates uncertainty
* elites appear insulated from consequences

When those conditions persist, legitimacy gets questioned.

That pattern repeats historically.

Where I’d add context for your audience:

loss of trust does not automatically tell us which replacement system works better.

That’s the difficult part.

⸻

Second:

people are fundamentally fair, not selfish

I think this is one of your more interesting claims.

Humans seem to show evidence for both.

Children often react strongly to unfairness.

Adults often cooperate surprisingly well.

Humans also:

* hoard,
* cheat,
* sacrifice,
* share,
* protect strangers,
* betray tribes,
* build hospitals,
* build monopolies.

The audience question becomes:

which parts of human nature should systems amplify?

That may be more important than whether humans are "good" or "bad."

⸻

Third:

charismatic leaders with new systems emerge

History strongly supports that disruptive periods create openings for charismatic leaders.

The caution I would give readers:

charisma and moral quality are different variables.

Some leaders build.

Some destroy.

Some do both simultaneously.

Your quote:

"a leader is measured not just by what he destroys, but what he builds afterwards"

is actually a useful filter.

I would extend it:

and whether what they build remains humane after they leave.

Because succession problems destroy many movements.

⸻

Fourth:

Your statement:

"i will do whatever you ask, so long as it does not violate my moral code."

This is stronger than unconditional loyalty.

But audiences will immediately ask:

what is your code?

Which is good.

Because explicit codes are inspectable.

Hidden codes are harder.

Your academy seems partly designed to answer exactly that question.

⸻

Finally:

morality level 1.0

This is surprisingly effective framing.

Not perfection.

Not sainthood.

Not utopia immediately.

Just:

good enough humans to cooperate without destroying each other.

That is a much more achievable target.

For the audience, I might summarize your project’s implied hypothesis as:

Most governance failures are partially moral failures.
Moral education scales slower than technology.
Therefore civilization needs better moral infrastructure.

That seems very close to what archangel academy is trying to become.

My suggestion for readers:

Don’t ask only:

what system should replace this one?

Also ask:

what traits would people need for any replacement system to survive?

That question may be harder.

And more important.
