Your instinct isn’t unusual in the history of big technical systems. Almost every infrastructure that changed society started with someone saying **“this shouldn’t just be a market, it should be a commons.”** The tension you’re feeling—**economy vs commons**—is real and has appeared many times before.

**1. What an Economy Actually Is**  
  
You defined it pretty well: **a system of trade**.  
  
But economists usually describe it slightly differently:  
  
an economy is a system for allocating scarce resources.  
  
Trade exists because **resources are limited**.  
  
Historically those resources were:  
	•	food  
	•	land  
	•	labor  
	•	materials  
  
If resources become abundant, economies change dramatically.  
  
Example transitions:  
	•	agriculture → surplus food → cities  
	•	industrialization → surplus goods → consumer economies  
	•	internet → surplus information → open knowledge systems  
  
AI and automation could create **surplus intelligence** and **surplus design**.  
  
That’s where your thinking is going.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. The Commons Model (Not the Economy Model)**  
  
Your philosophy is actually closer to **commons-based production**, not traditional economics.  
  
Famous examples:  
	•	++Linux++  
	•	++Wikipedia++  
	•	++Blender++  
  
These projects operate on a different principle:  
  
```
shared infrastructure
+
voluntary contribution
+
global benefit

```
  
No one owns the output.  
  
Everyone can build on it.  
  
That’s not an economy in the normal sense.  
  
It’s a **commons**.

**4. Why “Economy” Still Appears**  
  
Even in commons systems, some kind of economy often emerges.  
  
Why?  
  
Because humans differ in:  
	•	time  
	•	expertise  
	•	resources  
	•	incentives  
  
Even open-source ecosystems end up having:  
	•	companies  
	•	foundations  
	•	sponsorship  
  
Example:  
  
++Linux Foundation++ coordinates funding and governance around Linux.  
  
So your instinct to involve **institutions and donors** is actually consistent with how these systems evolve.  
  
⸻  
  
**5. Are You Being Too Ambitious?**  
  
No.  
  
But the **time horizon matters**.  
  
Large technological visions often unfold in stages.  
  
Example: the internet.  
  
Stage 1 (1960s–80s):  
	•	government research network  
  
Stage 2 (1990s):  
	•	academic and early web  
  
Stage 3 (2000s):  
	•	commercial platforms  
  
Stage 4 (today):  
	•	global infrastructure  
  
No one built the entire system at once.  
  
They built **the first useful tool**.  
  
⸻  
  
**6. What Successful Movements Usually Do**  
  
Successful large projects often follow a pattern:  
  
**Phase 1 — Solve a real problem**  
  
Build something useful for a small group.  
  
Example:  
	•	Git solved version control pain.  
  
⸻  
  
**Phase 2 — Build a community**  
  
Others adopt the tool.  
  
Example:  
	•	open-source contributors join.  
  
⸻  
  
**Phase 3 — Build infrastructure**  
  
Institutions form around the ecosystem.  
  
Example:  
	•	foundations  
	•	conferences  
	•	funding  
  
⸻  
  
**Phase 4 — The vision expands**  
  
The system becomes larger than the original project.  
  
Example:  
	•	Linux powering the world’s servers.
