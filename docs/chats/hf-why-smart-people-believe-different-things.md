# Why Smart People Believe Different Things
### Heavenfleet Lecture 11
### By Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink
Cadets,
Before discussing evidence, testimony, science, religion, politics, or philosophy, we must discuss definitions.
Many disagreements begin long before evidence is presented.
Many disagreements begin with words.
Commander Michael once considered two spools.
The first was a spool of red ribbon.
The second was a spool of magnet wire insulated in red.
At first glance they appeared similar.
Both were:
- long
- thin
- bendy
- red
- wrapped around a spool
Suppose we define a spool of red ribbon as:
> A long, thin, bendy red thing wrapped around a spool.
Under that definition, both objects qualify.
Yet most people would object immediately.
One is ribbon.
One is wire.
The disagreement is not about the object.
The disagreement is about the definition.
This is the Red Ribbon Problem.
People often believe they are arguing about reality when they are actually arguing about words.
---
# Definitions Come First
When two intelligent people disagree, one of the first questions Heavenfleet asks is:
> Are our definitions the same?
This question is surprisingly powerful.
Consider:
- freedom
- justice
- intelligence
- consciousness
- evidence
- science
- God
- heaven
- truth
Two people may use the same word while meaning entirely different things.
The conversation appears productive.
The disagreement appears real.
Yet both participants may be debating different subjects.
---
# The Hidden Source Of Disagreement
A common mistake among humans is believing:
> If another person disagrees with me, they must be stupid, evil, or lying.
This hypothesis fails quickly.
Many intelligent people disagree strongly.
Why?
Because intelligence is only one variable among many.
Humans are shaped by:
- family
- culture
- language
- definitions
- trusted authorities
- rewards and punishments
- personal experiences
- communities
- testimony
- fear
- hope
- timing
- luck
Years before two people meet, their models of reality are already diverging.
---
# Humans Inherit Stories
Imagine two children.
One grows up hearing:
> Creation explains the world.
Another grows up hearing:
> Evolution explains the world.
Each learns definitions.
Each learns trusted sources.
Each learns which questions are encouraged.
Each learns which questions are discouraged.
Years later they meet and call each other idiots.
Heavenfleet suspects the disagreement began much earlier.
Humans rarely inherit rebuttal trees.
Humans inherit coherent stories.
---
# Understanding Is Not Agreement
This realization leads to an important principle.
Understanding a belief does not require agreeing with it.
Compassion is not surrender.
Listening is not conversion.
Steelmanning is not betrayal.
A Heavenfleet Cadet should rarely ask:
> How do I destroy this belief?
Instead ask:
> How did this belief become possible?
This question often reveals more than a hundred arguments.
---
# The Simulation
Cadets are encouraged to run the following simulation.
Imagine you were them.
Not metaphorically.
Literally.
Same parents.
Same teachers.
Same experiences.
Same fears.
Same incentives.
Same heroes.
Same pain.
Would you still become you?
The answer is less obvious than many people expect.
---
# Identity And Understanding
Many beliefs become attached to identity.
This creates problems.
A rigid identity often reduces compassion.
Because if you cannot imagine becoming another person, you cannot imagine understanding them.
Labels are useful.
Sometimes.
Dangerous.
Sometimes.
Commander Michael once observed:
> Identity fades as one becomes wiser.
Heavenfleet currently treats labels as tools.
Not gods.
---
# Final Protocol
When encountering disagreement:
1. Learn the definitions.
2. Learn where the beliefs came from.
3. Study the testimony.
4. Review the evidence.
5. Separate people from claims.
6. Judge slowly.
7. Be an angel about it.
---
# Final Notes From Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink
Smart people disagree.
Not because intelligence is rare.
Because experiences differ.
Definitions differ.
Assumptions differ.
Stories differ.
The hopeful skeptic understands this.
The cynic sees disagreement and concludes:
> One side must be stupid.
The Heavenfleet Cadet sees disagreement and asks:
> What definitions are being used?
And:
> How did these people arrive here?
For many conflicts begin not with evidence.
Not with testimony.
Not with truth.
But with a spool of red ribbon that may not be ribbon at all.
