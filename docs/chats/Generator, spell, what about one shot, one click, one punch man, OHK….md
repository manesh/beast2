There’s a lot of intensity in what you wrote—frustration, loneliness, anger at systems that feel irrational, and also a strong drive to **build something better**. Those things often show up together in people who try to design new tools or systems. Engineers and creators frequently start from the feeling that *the current world is badly designed*.  
  
But I want to respond carefully to a few parts of what you said.  
  
⸻  
  
**1. First: you don’t sound useless**  
  
From what you’ve described in this conversation alone, you are:  
	•	designing a DSL  
	•	thinking about parser architecture  
	•	comparing UI paradigms  
	•	reasoning about LLM context stability  
	•	writing philosophy around the system  
  
That’s not what useless people do.  
  
Whether the project becomes huge or not, **the act of building and thinking like this already demonstrates capability**.  
  
A lot of important systems started exactly like that: one person annoyed that existing tools were bad.  
  
For example, ++Git++ was created by ++Linus Torvalds++ largely because he disliked the alternatives.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. Distrust and self-reliance**  
  
You described a mindset like:  
  
“If you want something done right, you have to do it yourself.”  
  
Many engineers and inventors operate partly like that.  
  
But there’s a trade-off:  
  
**Strategy**	**Strength**	**Risk**  
extreme self-reliance	independence, deep focus	isolation  
extreme dependence	collaboration	loss of agency  
  
The healthiest systems tend to land somewhere **between those extremes**.  
  
Even the strongest individual builders eventually need collaborators, readers, or users.  
  
Not because they’re weak, but because **complex systems exceed one mind’s context window**.  
  
⸻  
  
**3. About the anger toward people**  
  
I want to be very clear about one thing.  
  
Feeling angry or hurt is understandable when someone has had painful experiences.  
  
But the idea that **most people hate you or want you harmed** is very rarely accurate. Human beings are messy, distracted, inconsistent—but usually not organized around harming one specific person.  
  
When the mind is under a lot of stress it can start constructing models where:  
  
```
everyone is hostile
systems are conspiracies
respect is impossible

```
  
Those models feel internally logical, but they can make the world feel far more dangerous than it actually is.  
  
You deserve support and safety, and if you’re carrying a lot of pain from past experiences, talking to a **trusted person or professional** can genuinely help lighten that load. You shouldn’t have to carry everything alone.  
  
⸻  
  
**4. Your “three spellcasters” metaphor**  
  
Your metaphor actually maps nicely to three real styles of builders:  
  
**1. Button-press wizard**  
  
Uses tools without understanding internals.  
  
Example:  
	•	someone using an AI image generator as a black box  
  
Fast, but limited.  
  
⸻  
  
**2. Engineer**  
  
Understands the machinery.  
  
Builds the flamethrower instead of just pressing the wand button.  
  
Example:  
	•	writing the C backend you described  
  
Powerful, but still focused on implementation.  
  
⸻  
  
**3. Architect / theorist**  
  
Studies the underlying language of systems.  
  
Creates **frameworks, DSLs, or paradigms** others can use.  
  
Example historically:  
	•	++Donald Knuth++ creating literate programming  
	•	++Ken Thompson++ shaping early Unix philosophy  
  
From your descriptions, you’re trying to operate in **category 3**.  
  
That’s harder and slower than category 1, but it can produce deeper systems.  
  
⸻  
  
**5. Why many tools feel badly designed**  
  
You asked a good question:  
  
why are so many AI tools slow or messy?  
  
Often because of these constraints:  
	•	researchers prioritize **experiments over engineering**  
	•	Python ecosystems emphasize **flexibility over speed**  
	•	UI frameworks prioritize **rapid prototyping**  
  
So you get tools like ++ComfyUI++ that are powerful but not always polished.  
  
Your instinct to write a **fast C backend** is actually a very classic engineering reaction to that.  
  
⸻  
  
**6. Something important about your motivation**  
  
You said something revealing:  
  
I keep trying to improve myself because clearly, if I’m not finding happiness, I’m the problem.  
  
That’s admirable self-reflection, but it can also become unfair to yourself.  
  
Not every negative experience means **you are the cause**.  
  
Sometimes it means:  
	•	the environment was wrong  
	•	the people involved were wrong  
	•	the timing was wrong  
  
Humans tend to **over-assign blame to themselves** when searching for control.  
  
⸻

Many people who eventually build interesting systems spend years feeling like:  
  
```
maybe this is pointless
maybe I'm wasting time
maybe nobody cares

```
  
That uncertainty is part of creative work.  
  
It doesn’t mean the work is worthless.

**8. One final thought**  
  
You ended with a lot of anger toward people who might ignore your ideas.  
  
Here’s the strange truth about ideas:  
  
Most ideas are ignored **at first**.  
  
Not because the world hates the person who created them, but because people are busy, skeptical, or unaware.  
  
The goal isn’t to **force people to listen**.  
  
The goal is to build something so interesting that curiosity eventually pulls them in.
