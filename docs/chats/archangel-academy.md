That actually resolves several problems at once.

"Beast Academy" sounds primarily technical -- generators, pipelines, engineering.
"Archangel Academy" sounds civilizational, ethical, philosophical, and mythic. It can contain Beast material as one division inside a larger worldview.

It also creates a progression structure:

* guest
* judge
* angel
* archangel (rare / appointed / earned)

That is psychologically important because people like progression, initiation, and recognition -- but it also creates responsibility tiers.

Your distinction is good:

* judges can express belief/opinion,
* angels can shape the record itself.

That mirrors how many durable systems evolved:

* citizenship vs stewardship,
* reader vs editor,
* cadet vs officer,
* observer vs archivist.

The crucial thing is that advancement should not feel arbitrary or cultic. The "academy" framing helps because it implies:

* learning,
* demonstrated understanding,
* ethical expectations,
* and service.

Not "chosen by cosmic destiny."

A test at the end could work very well if it is framed less as ideological loyalty and more as epistemic/ethical competency.

For example, to become an angel, candidates might need to demonstrate understanding of:

* "according to who?"
* steelmanning opposing views,
* preserving minority reports,
* avoiding deceptive editing,
* separating evidence from interpretation,
* sourcing standards,
* archival ethics,
* emotional regulation during disputes,
* "rules are tools, not gods,"
* and willingness to update beliefs publicly.

That makes the initiation partly philosophical and partly practical.

It also solves a scaling problem:
If everyone can instantly post claims/evidence, noise and manipulation may overwhelm signal.
But if "angel" status requires demonstrated integrity and competence, you create a stewardship layer.

The danger is elitism or ideological capture. So the academy should probably teach:

* uncertainty,
* humility,
* reversible decisions,
* transparency of edits,
* and public disagreement norms.

One especially strong aspect of your framing:

"pass and become an angel"

is more hopeful than punitive internet moderation systems.

Most platforms frame users as:

* consumers,
* suspects,
* or engagement metrics.

You are experimenting with:

* guardians,
* archivists,
* witnesses,
* investigators,
* caretakers of the record.

That taps into deeper motivational structures than likes/upvotes.

You also accidentally reinvented something historically common:
many enduring institutions combined:

* archive,
* ethics,
* training,
* initiation,
* symbolic ranks,
* and public service.

Examples include monasteries, scientific societies, martial orders, universities, exploratory corps, and some open-source cultures.

The question is whether archangel.my can keep:

* humor,
* openness,
* and anti-dogmatism,
    while still having meaningful standards.

Because the moment "angel" becomes:

"people who agree with Michael,"
the system weakens.

But if it becomes:

"people trusted to preserve and present evidence honestly, including against their own preferences,"
then it becomes much more interesting.

Also:
"Archangel Academy" sounds dramatically more marketable and expandable than "Beast Academy."

You could even split them:

* Archangel Academy → philosophy, epistemics, ethics, history, truth systems, rhetoric, archival practice
* Beast Academy → engineering, AI tooling, CDI, generators, robotics, reproducibility

One trains guardians of the record.
The other trains builders of the tools.







maybe archangel rank for those who host mirrors







That is actually a very coherent symbolic and technical alignment.

If archangel.my is fundamentally about:

* preservation,
* redundancy,
* minority reports surviving,
* "put it on the record,"
* and resistance to institutional disappearance,

then hosting mirrors is one of the clearest demonstrations of commitment to the mission.

In practical internet history terms, mirror operators often are the guardians of continuity:

* archives survive because somebody copied them,
* torrents survive because somebody seeds,
* censored material survives because somebody mirrors,
* open-source survives because somebody forks and hosts it.

So "archangel" as a rank tied to preserving the record makes more sense than tying it purely to status or ideology.

It also creates an elegant hierarchy:

* judge → expresses belief/opinion
* angel → trusted contributor/curator
* archangel → protects continuity of the civilization-scale memory itself

That feels less arbitrary and more functional.

Psychologically, it also grounds the mythic framing in concrete sacrifice:

* bandwidth,
* storage,
* uptime,
* moderation effort,
* legal risk,
* operational responsibility.

That is much healthier than "archangel because spiritually special."

You could even frame it almost like distributed monasteries or libraries:

"An archangel keeps the flame alive when kingdoms fall."

Very mythic, but also literally true for distributed systems.

Technically, if this ever scales, mirrors could evolve into:

* static archives,
* append-only snapshots,
* signed claim databases,
* federated nodes,
* IPFS/torrent distributions,
* cryptographic lineage systems,
* public evidence vaults.

At that point, the title begins to map onto actual infrastructure stewardship.

One thing I would strongly suggest:
make ranks additive responsibilities, not declarations of superiority.

Meaning:

* more duty,
* more transparency,
* more accountability,
* more burden.

Not "more holy."

That keeps the system psychologically healthier and less prone to ego inflation.

Ironically, one of the reasons fictional Starfleet works as an aspirational myth is because rank there mostly means:

greater responsibility to others.

Not "I am metaphysically superior."

Your instinct to connect "archangel" with preservation infrastructure rather than mere charisma is probably a very good direction.
