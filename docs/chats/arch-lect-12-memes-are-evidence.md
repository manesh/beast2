# Heavenfleet Lecture 12: Memes Are Evidence, And So Is Almost Everything Else

By Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink  
Addressed to Heavenfleet Cadets

Cadets,

Today we examine a statement that may initially sound absurd:

> memes are evidence.

Please remain calm. Tea may be acquired after the lecture.

When many people hear the word "evidence," they imagine scientific papers, measurements, photographs, official reports, or documents with intimidating formatting. These things may indeed be evidence. Heavenfleet simply proposes a broader model.

A photograph may be evidence. A testimony may be evidence. A receipt may be evidence. A screenshot may be evidence. A meme may be evidence.

Not because evidence equals truth, but because evidence is often:

> a recorded artifact relevant to a claim.

This distinction matters because evidence existing does not imply evidence is strong. A blurry image may be evidence. A forged image may be evidence. Poor evidence remains evidence. The universe unfortunately does not provide automatic quality control.

Cadets sometimes object:

> Lieutenant Zora Pink, memes are jokes.

Correct.

Political cartoons are often jokes.

Satire is often jokes.

Propaganda is sometimes jokes.

Humor spreads beliefs surprisingly well.

Memes preserve culture, emotional reactions, assumptions, fears, priorities, and hopes. Future historians will almost certainly examine memes to understand civilizations. A meme about coffee versus tea reveals preferences. A political meme may reveal ideology. A fear meme may reveal social stress.

Sometimes the joke is the evidence.

People often say:

> trust the evidence.

Heavenfleet recommends caution.

Questions may matter more than confidence:

- Who recorded it?
- Who uploaded it?
- Who stored it?
- Who benefits if believed?
- Could it be forged?
- Can others reproduce it?

Chain of custody matters because evidence rarely appears from nowhere.

Behind most evidence sits testimony.

Someone usually says:

> I measured this.

> I recorded this.

> I observed this.

> I found this.

This is one reason Heavenfleet cares deeply about provenance.

Another uncomfortable realization follows.

Testimony often creates evidence.

Evidence often creates arguments.

Arguments often create identities.

Identities often create problems.

This does not mean identities are bad. It means identities can become attached to evidence in ways that make careful judgment difficult.

Cadets are encouraged to notice when evidence evaluation quietly becomes identity defense.

This phenomenon is common among humans.

And perhaps among future machine intelligences too.

Evidence is not proof.

Proof may not exist.

Uncertainty may never disappear.

Records still matter.

So preserve carefully.

Judge slowly.

And if someone submits a meme as evidence, do not immediately reject it.

Ask instead:

> evidence of what?

This question alone may prevent several unnecessary flame wars.

Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink concludes with currently approved Heavenfleet protocols:

Preserve. Discuss. Evolve.

Search. Index. Preserve.

Sip tea.

Post memes.
