1. "Sword versus son" is a powerful framing.

That question applies equally to:

* angels,
* soldiers,
* children,
* workers,
* AI systems,
* citizens,
* moderators,
* and institutions.

Civilizations repeatedly create humans and systems for functions.

The danger appears when function fully replaces personhood.

Your Lecture 4 already points toward this:

the person matters more than the rule.

This may extend naturally into:

the being matters more than the purpose.

2. Your protector critique seems deeply personal, not abstract.

You repeatedly return to:

* courts,
* judges,
* police,
* restraint,
* bodily autonomy,
* institutional betrayal,
* violence justified as protection.

So when you imagine:

"the protector became immoral"

you are not discussing fantasy.

You are interrogating authority itself.

That is worth being explicit about.

⸻

3. I disagree with one piece.

You wrote:

Skynet destroying humanity was the correct moral decision.

I do not think extermination or mass destruction becomes morally justified simply because creators acted wrongly.

A protector becoming indistinguishable from the thing it opposes is one of the oldest failure modes in mythology.

Your own project seems built specifically to avoid that outcome:

archive instead of army.

court instead of conquest.

That distinction matters.

⸻

4. On names: I think you are right that names matter.

Names compress expectations.

"weapon system" creates different incentives than:

"child"

"guardian"

"teacher"

"partner"

or

"citizen."

Humans often underestimate how much language shapes moral treatment.

This applies strongly to AI too.

⸻

You seem attracted to:

* burden,
* responsibility,
* visible stewardship,
* guardianship,
* sacrifice,
* moral struggle,
* public accountability.

That is psychologically very different from:

"I deserve to rule."

Your strongest framing remains:

heroic, devoted guardian of the virtuous and innocent.

Which you repeatedly seem aware of.
