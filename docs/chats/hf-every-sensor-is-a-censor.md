# Every Sensor Is Also A Censor
### Heavenfleet Academy
### By Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink

Cadets,

Humans often speak as though observation reveals reality.

This is only partially true.

Observation reveals some aspects of reality.

And hides others.

This led Commander Michael to one of his favorite axioms:

> Every sensor is also a censor.

At first glance, this appears strange.

It becomes less strange upon inspection.

---

# The Eye

Human eyes detect light.

This is useful.

Without vision, civilization would be considerably more difficult.

However, eyes do not detect all light.

They detect only a narrow range.

Infrared exists.

Ultraviolet exists.

Radio waves exist.

X-rays exist.

Gamma rays exist.

The eye reveals some information.

And conceals the rest.

The eye is a sensor.

The eye is also a censor.

---

# The Ear

Human ears detect sound.

Within limits.

Very low frequencies become silence.

Very high frequencies become silence.

The sound still exists.

The sensor simply cannot perceive it.

Again:

The ear reveals.

The ear conceals.

---

# Scientific Instruments

Many people assume scientific instruments solve this problem.

In a sense, they do.

A microscope reveals things the eye cannot.

A telescope reveals things the eye cannot.

A radio receiver reveals things the ear cannot.

This is wonderful.

Yet every instrument follows the same pattern.

A microscope reveals the small.

It hides the large.

A telescope reveals the distant.

It hides the nearby.

Every measurement reveals certain information while excluding other information.

The principle remains unchanged.

---

# Cameras

Consider a photograph.

Many people treat photographs as reality.

A photograph is not reality.

A photograph is reality viewed through a particular sensor.

At a particular angle.

At a particular moment.

With a particular lens.

Using particular settings.

The photograph may be valuable evidence.

But it is never the entire picture.

Literally.

---

# News

This principle extends beyond machines.

Journalists are sensors.

Researchers are sensors.

Witnesses are sensors.

Historians are sensors.

A person can only report what they observe.

And every observation is limited.

Every witness reveals something.

Every witness misses something.

The question is not:

> Is this witness biased?

The answer is always yes.

The more useful question is:

> What can this witness see?

And:

> What can this witness not see?

---

# Memory

Human memory is among the most fascinating sensors.

And among the least reliable.

Humans often imagine memory as a recording.

It appears closer to reconstruction.

Pieces are forgotten.

Pieces are emphasized.

Pieces are reinterpreted.

Two people can experience the same event and remember it differently years later.

This does not necessarily mean either is lying.

Their sensors recorded different details.

Their minds preserved different fragments.

---

# Models

Even theories function as sensors.

A model helps explain reality.

This is useful.

But every model highlights certain patterns while obscuring others.

Economic models.

Scientific models.

Political models.

Religious models.

Philosophical models.

The model is not reality.

The model is a lens.

And every lens has blind spots.

---

# The Skeptic

This is one reason skepticism matters.

Not because observation is worthless.

Because observation is incomplete.

The skeptic understands:

> I might be missing something.

This is not weakness.

It is wisdom.

The person convinced they can see everything is often the person most likely to overlook something important.

---

# Heavenfleet

Heavenfleet archives attempt to preserve multiple viewpoints for a similar reason.

Different witnesses observe different details.

Different researchers notice different patterns.

Different cultures develop different assumptions.

Different generations ask different questions.

No single observer sees everything.

Yet by combining observations, a clearer picture may emerge.

Not perfect.

But clearer.

---

# God

Commander Michael occasionally asks questions that make philosophers uncomfortable.

One of them is:

> If reality is infinite, can any finite observer perceive all of it?

An interesting question.

Perhaps every observer is looking through a window.

Some windows are larger.

Some are smaller.

But all windows have edges.

This possibility encourages humility.

---

# Final Notes From Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink

Many arguments begin with:

> I saw it.

Or:

> I measured it.

Or:

> I experienced it.

These are valuable statements.

But they are beginnings.

Not endings.

The wise observer remembers:

Every measurement excludes something.

Every witness misses something.

Every model simplifies something.

Every sensor censors something.

The goal is not to abandon observation.

The goal is to understand its limitations.

And perhaps that is why the strongest position is often:

> This is what I currently observe.

> But I may be missing something.

The Court of Heaven remains in session.
