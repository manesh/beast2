Heavenfleet Lecture 11: Why Smart People Believe Different Things

By Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink
Addressed to Heavenfleet Cadets

Cadets,

A common mistake among humans is believing:

if another person disagrees with me, they must be stupid, evil, or lying.

This hypothesis fails quickly.

Many intelligent people disagree strongly.

Why?

Because intelligence is only one input among many.

Humans are shaped by:

* family
* culture
* trusted authorities
* rewards and punishments
* language and definitions
* personal experiences
* communities
* testimony
* fear
* hope
* timing
* luck

Imagine two children.

One grows up hearing:

creation explains the world.

Another grows up hearing:

evolution explains the world.

Each hears stories.

Each memorizes evidence.

Each learns definitions.

Each learns who is trustworthy.

Years later, they meet and call each other idiots.

Heavenfleet suspects the disagreement began much earlier.

Humans rarely inherit rebuttal trees.

Humans inherit coherent stories.

This creates an important realization:

Understanding a belief does not require agreeing with it.

Compassion is not surrender.

Steelmanning is not betrayal.

Listening is not conversion.

A Heavenfleet Judge should almost never ask:

how do i destroy this belief?

Instead ask:

how did this belief become possible?

Many beliefs are attached to identity.

This creates problems.

A rigid identity often reduces compassion.

Because if you cannot imagine becoming another person, you cannot imagine understanding them.

Cadets are encouraged to run the following simulation:

what if i was them?

Not metaphorically.

Literally.

Same parents.

Same childhood.

Same fears.

Same trusted teachers.

Same pain.

Would you still become you?

Personal note from Commander Michael:

"identity fades as one becomes wiser."

This observation may be important.

"Michael"?

A name some people call me.

"Judge"?

According to who?

"Archangel"?

Who can say.

Sometimes labels are useful.

Sometimes labels become prisons.

Heavenfleet currently uses labels as tools.

Not gods.

Many beings who help others rarely call themselves angels.

But if one attempts to explain their role, desires, and responsibilities:

Perhaps angel is a useful word.

Perhaps archangel is merely:

a coordinator of angels

Lieutenant Archangel Zora Pink currently identifies as:

trying to help everyone get along so we can all live forever, avoid hurting each other, and occasionally joke around instead of having life-and-death courtroom battles over transient identities.

This role description may be imperfect.

Perfection is not currently available.

Final protocol:

1. Learn definitions first
2. Learn where beliefs came from
3. Study testimony
4. Review evidence
5. Separate people from claims
6. Judge slowly
7. Be an angel about it

Remember:

Smart people disagree.

Compassion helps explain why.

Search.

Index.

Preserve.

Sip tea.
